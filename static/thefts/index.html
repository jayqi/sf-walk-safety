SF Walk Safety

A data-driven examination of pedestrian safety in San Francisco.

Thefts

Thefts are the crime of stealing possessions. Here, we consider street-level cases of theft that involve pedestrians, including pickpocketing and pursesnatching. Using open data from the San Francisco Police department, we visualize street theft incidents since 2003 and examine their prevalence.

From 2003 through 2015, there were a total of 63,672 street theft reports, for an average of 13.4 in the city each day. In the adjacent figure, we see the total number of reported street theft incidents each year from 2003 to 2015. The number of yearly reports from 2012 onwards are nearly double that of earlier years.

Analysis

Below are some detailed analysis of how thefts relate to other variables. Unless otherwise specified, the analysis in this section only considers theft reports from 2003 through 2015, where we have full years.

Time of Day

The adjacent histogram shows (normalized) frequency of thefts by hour of the day. We can see that there is quite a strong daily pattern, with the low point happening in the hours before dawn, the maximum happening in the afternoon, and a smaller second peak around midnight.

Plotted as a dark blue curve is a temporal probability distribution model for the thefts, generated using kernel density estimation. This distribution predicts the likelihood that, if we consider a theft, when during the day that it occurs.

By Weekday

In the preceding figure, the average thefts per day for each day of the week is shown. The blue lines show one standard deviation above and below, to give an idea of the amount of variation.

We can see that Mondays tend to have fewer thefts, and that thefts increase over the week. The most thefts happen on the weekend, especially Friday and Saturday.

By Month

The next plot shows the number of thefts each month, averaged across the years from 2003 to 2015. The envelope is one standard deviation above and below.

It seems that there tends to be slightly more thefts in the late summer, while fewer in the winter. However, the difference is fairly small, especially compared to the amount of variation across years.

Rain

The preceding histogram shows frequency of days where it rained a certain amount. Most days in San Francisco are dry, and there are very few days at high amounts of rain over the last thirteen years.

The next plot shows the number of thefts per day averaged across days of similar amounts of rain. On the left side where we have bins with many observations, we can see that as it rains more, the number of thefts tends to be a little less. This is also reflected in the linear fit in the following figure. However, the rain only captures a very small amount of the variation, as the correlation coefficient is only 0.011.

Temperature

The next two figures show the number of thefts each day, averaged across days of similar daily low temperature and daily high temperature, respectively. This shows that colder days have progressively fewer thefts than warmer days. Overall, however, this effect is small compared to variation across days. The days at the extremes on either side are not necessarily representative, because there are very few observed days at those temperatures.

About the data

The theft reports come from the San Francisco Police Department Incident Reporting dataset on the SF Open Data portal. Learn more about this dataset as well as the others here.
