Project Highlights

Here you can see some of the interesting highlights from our analysis and modeling. To see more exhaustive results, visit the main pages for thefts, robberies, and traffic collisions.

Day of Week

Thefts occur much more frequently on the weekend, especially Friday and Saturday.

Robberies also occur more frequently on the weekend, though the effect is much smaller.

Time of Day Distribution

Histograms showing respectively theft incidents and robbery incidents binned by hour of the day. Also plotted are probability density functions estimated using gaussian kernel density estimation. The so-called Silverman's rule for optimal bandwidth choice is used.

Thefts are most frequent in the afternoon and evening, with a smaller second peak at late hours of the night. Their least frequent time is the early hours of the morning.

In contrast, robberies are at their least frequent in the late morning and at their most frequent in the late hours of the night.

Weather

Below are plots showing the number of thefts each day against the daily high and low temperatures. It appears that on colder days, there are fewer thefts.

The extreme ends of the range do not have many observations (<100 days) so those values are less indicative.




Interestingly, robberies do not show such a trend. Even though robberies happen overwhelmingly at night, there does not appear to be correlation with temperatures.

Spatial Kernel Density Estimates

Below are maps showing spatial probability distributions for thefts and robberies, respectively, computed using kernel density estimation. The way to interpret these distributions is as follows: if a theft or robbery incident occurs, what is the probability it occurs in a certain part of the city. The intensity values are scaled to the maximum value of the respective distributions, and are plotted by default with logarithmic scaling to enhance the lower value features.

We can see that for both thefts and robberies, downtown along Market Street is where the incidents are most likely to occur. The peaks are not quite in the same place—the peak for robberies is in Tenderloin while the peak for thefts is shifted more into the Financial District. For both types of incidents, local pockets of higher probability are spread across the city.
