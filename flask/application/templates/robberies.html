Robberies

Robberies are crimes of taking possessions by force or threat of force. Here, we consider street-level cases of robbery that involve pedestrians, such as muggings. Using open data from the San Francisco Police department, we visualize street robbery incidents since 2003 and examine their prevalence.

From 2003 through 2015, there were a total of 24,889 street robbery reports, for an average of 5.2 in the city each day. In the adjacent figure, we see the total number of reported street robbery incidents each year from 2003 to 2015. The number of yearly reports of robberies peaked in 2006 and then as since generally been decreasing.

Analysis

Below are some detailed analysis of how robberies relate to other variables. Unless otherwise specified, the analysis in this section only considers robbery reports from 2003 through 2015, where we have full years.

Time of Day

The adjacent histogram shows (normalized) frequency of robberies by hour of the day. We can see that there is quite a strong daily pattern. The least number of robberies happen during the morning commute hours, while the most happen around midnight.

Plotted as a dark blue curve is a temporal probability distribution model for the robberies, generated using kernel density estimation. This distribution predicts the likelihood that, if we consider a robbery, when during the day that it occurs.

By Weekday

In the preceding figure, the average robberies per day for each day of the week is shown. The blue lines show one standard deviation above and below, to give an idea of the amount of variation.

Robbery prevalence appears to be independent of the day of week.

By Month

The next plot shows the number of robberies each month, averaged across the years from 2003 to 2015. The envelope is one standard deviation above and below.

It seems that there tends to be slightly more robberies in the late summer, while fewer in the winter. However, the difference is fairly small, especially compared to the amount of variation across years.

Rain

The preceding histogram shows frequency of days where it rained a certain amount. Most days in San Francisco are dry, and there are very few days at high amounts of rain over the last thirteen years.

Unlike thefts, the is no significant relationship between rain and frequency of robberies.

Temperature

The next two figures show the number of robberies each day, averaged across days of similar daily low temperature and daily high temperature, respectively. Unlike thefts, there appears to be no significant relationship between the daily temperatures and the frequency of robberies.

About the data

The robbery reports come from the San Francisco Police Department Incident Reporting dataset on the SF Open Data portal. Learn more about this dataset as well as the others here.
